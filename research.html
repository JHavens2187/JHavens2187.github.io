The Challenge: Seeing Through Cosmic Dust

To understand the first galaxies and their supermassive black holes (AGN), we must first understand the dust that obscures them. A promising method to measure the "attenuation law" is to compare hydrogen line ratios (e.g., $H\alpha$, $H\beta$, $Pa\beta$).

This method has been notoriously difficult. As shown in Prescott et al. (2022), ground-based attempts suffer from severe biases like "slit losses," making the results unreliable. The paper concluded that a single, powerful instrument that can capture all lines at once—like JWST—is required.

My Research: Using JWST & GLEAM to Solve the Puzzle

My research, as part of Dr. Kirkpatrick's group, is one of the first to apply this "three-line method" using new, high-quality data from the JADES and SMACS JWST surveys.

To measure the lines, I am using and testing the GLEAM fitting tool. My work involves running GLEAM on the JWST spectra to robustly fit the key emission lines and measure their flux ratios. This allows us to constrain the dust attenuation law (both $n$ and $\tau_V$) free from the biases of ground-based data.

Next Steps: Building a More Complete Model

This project's "very recent addition" is to use Star Formation Rates (SFRs) as an independent constraint. This is a cutting-edge field; for example, Shapley et al. (2023) used JWST to get dust-corrected $H\alpha$-based SFRs.

By combining our dust measurements (like the y-axis in the plot) with independent SFRs, we can break model degeneracies. The future of this work is to build a complete physical model connecting the AGN activity, star formation, and dust in these early-universe galaxies.
