The Challenge: Seeing Through Cosmic Dust

To understand the properties of the first galaxies and their supermassive black holes (AGN), we must first understand the dust that obscures them. Dust doesn't block all light equallyâ€”it follows an "attenuation law." A promising method to measure this law is to compare the ratios of multiple hydrogen emission lines (e.g., Hα, Hβ, and a Paschen line).

This method, however, has been notoriously difficult. As shown in Prescott et al. (2022), ground-based attempts suffer from severe biases. Because different lines are observed with different telescopes at different times, you get "slit losses" and calibration errors, making the results unreliable. This is precisely the capability that JWST now provides.

My Research: Using JWST & GLEAM to Solve the Puzzle

My research, as part of Dr. Kirkpatrick's group, is one of the first to apply this "three-line method" using new, high-quality data from the JADES (JWST Advanced Deep Extragalactic Survey) and SMACS (Southern MAssive Cluster Survey) programs.

To measure the lines, I am using and testing the GLEAM fitting tool. My work involves running GLEAM on the JWST spectra to robustly fit the key emission lines and measure their flux ratios. From these ratios, we can constrain the dust attenuation law (both its slope, $n$, and normalization, $\tau_V$) in these early-universe AGN, free from the biases of ground-based data.

Next Steps: Building a More Complete Model

The "very recent addition" to this project is to use Star Formation Rates (SFRs) as an independent constraint. Measuring dust properties from line ratios alone can sometimes be "degenerate," where different models produce similar-looking results.

By adding an independent measurement (the galaxy's total SFR, which is also affected by dust), we can break these degeneracies. The future of this work is to combine our GLEAM-derived dust measurements with SFRs to build a complete physical model connecting the AGN activity, the star formation in its host galaxy, and the dust that fuels and obscures them both.
